# Task 1: AI Evidence Dossier (Group) – Phased Due Dates

The goal of next week's session is to move beyond "opinion-based" talk and become an **expert advocate** for specific perspectives on AI in research and learning. In next week's session, we are going to have a group **"Evidence Arena"** on the topic of *"Using AI for Learning and Research"*.

Each group is to research the topic in preparation for the discussion, compiling an **AI Evidence Dossier**. During the session, groups will argue their points on specific questions. The catch is that the groups must be able to back up their arguments based on evidence/facts from their Dossier. **If it cannot be backed up, the argument is invalid…**

---

## Phase 1: Pre-Session Preparation – Compiling an Evidence Dossier *(due next week)*

As a group, you are to compile an Evidence Dossier in preparation for next week's session. This Dossier is due next week prior to the session. It must be submitted on Moodle.

### 1. Research Scoping

Investigate how AI is impacting learning (e.g. personalised tutors, accessibility, skill acquisition) and research (e.g. data analysis, automated writing, ethics).

### 2. Submit 2 Discussion Provocations

Provide two "tough questions" or controversial statements you want the class to tackle[^1].

- **Example A (The Accountability Dilemma):**
  > "If an AI tool is used to assist in a systematic literature review and it misses a key set of papers due to algorithmic bias, who is responsible for the 'scientific error'—the student, the software developer, or the supervisor?"

- **Example B (The Skill-Loss Dilemma):**
  > "As AI becomes more capable of generating code and data visualizations, should universities stop teaching the 'basics' of these skills to focus on 'high-level' AI management, or does 'outsourcing' the basics destroy a student's ability to understand the results?"

- **Example C (The Technical Efficacy Dilemma):**
  > "Research into 'Chain-of-Thought' prompting suggests that asking an AI to 'think step-by-step' improves its reasoning. However, in a research context, does this technique make the user more likely to trust a 'logical-sounding' but false conclusion? How do we validate the technique's output?"

[^1]: The Evidence Arena will address specific, randomly chosen provocations, as submitted by the groups, as well as some unseen "curve-balls" selected by the Arena Master.

### 3. Build the Evidence Bank

Identify **5–8 high-quality sources** (journals, policy reports, or technical white papers). For each source, provide:

- **The Citation:** Full bibliographic detail.
- **A "Summary":** A short summary of the article, no more than one paragraph.
- **The "Data Points":** One or more specific facts, statistics, or ethical frameworks found in the text.
- **The Argument:** One–two sentences on how these facts support or challenge a specific use of AI.

### In-Class Expectation

Bring **two physical copies** (or one laptop) of this Dossier to class. You will be expected to cite these sources by name during the 8-minute topic **"Evidence Sprints"**[^2].

[^2]: See Phase 2 below.

---

## Phase 2: The In-Session "Evidence Arena"

During the classroom session, topics will be randomly selected.

- **The Rule of Evidence:** Any statement you make must be backed by your Dossier.
- **The Challenge:** If a peer or the Arena Master challenges your claim, you have **30 seconds** to provide the specific citation or data point.
- **Active Listening:** Take notes or use the provided session transcript; you will need to quote your peers in your final memo (see Phase 3).

### Evaluation Rubric

Consider the following when presenting evidence or making statements during the session:

| Category              | Emerging (Low)                                                        | Proficient (Target)                                              | Masterful (High)                                                        |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Evidence Usage**    | Makes general claims ("People say...") without specific backing.      | Cites a source by name/year to support a specific point.         | Seamlessly integrates data from the Dossier to counter a peer's point.  |
| **Response to Challenge** | Becomes defensive or cannot locate the source in their notes.     | Locates the source and clarifies the specific data point within 30s. | Uses a source to pivot the conversation to a deeper level of inquiry. |
| **Engagement**        | Passive; only speaks when called upon.                                | Actively contributes to at least 3 of the 6 topics.             | Connects ideas across different topics (e.g., "This links back to Topic 1..."). |
| **Information Literacy** | Relies on biased or "pop-science" blog posts.                      | Uses a mix of academic journals, policy reports, and news.       | Critically evaluates the limitations of their own sources during the talk. |

> **The aim is for proficient to masterful for the session. To achieve this, be sure to be well prepared.**
